# Cubby 2024 Brand Strategy & Design
## OVERVIEW

Food waste isn't a sustainability problem—it's a personal one. Americans throw away over 100 billion pounds annually, 39% from home. Households waste money, forget groceries, and don't know when food expires. It's lonely, frustrating, and nobody talks about it.

Cubby solves this by being someone you can rely on. Not preachy. Not guilt-driven. Just a trusted friend in your kitchen who helps you manage your food with care.

I partnered with Cubby to build a brand strategy and visual identity centered on trust and companionship—something distinctly different from a market oversaturated with shame-based sustainability messaging.

## STRATEGY

### The Market Problem

While many apps promise to solve food waste, they all rely on the same thing: making users feel bad for the environment. None address what actually matters to people—saving time, saving money, and keeping their kitchen organized.

### KEY INSIGHT

Only Cubby can position food management as a personal win, not a planetary burden. By shifting focus from environmental guilt to kitchen control, Cubby becomes the practical tool people actually want to use.

### SOLUTION

Cubby's brand positioning: **A Trusted Companion in Your Kitchen.** Not "save the planet." Not "reduce waste." Just the idea that you have someone—or something—reliable in your corner.

The insight was human. Cubby isn't a gadget that judges you. It's a friend who remembers what you have, knows when things expire, and has your back when you're standing in the grocery store wondering what you actually need. It's someone you can rely on.

I created a visual identity that reflected this trust: a friendly, smiley icon (turning a cold storage locker into something warm and human), bright green signaling growth and optimism, and warm orange adding approachability. The voice was conversational and kind—never preachy. The message was simple: *You've got a friend in your kitchen.*

## OUTCOME

The brand launched with integrated out-of-home placements and grassroots social activation. The response was immediate. People didn't just use Cubby—they felt understood by it.

The positioning resonated because Cubby wasn't asking people to save the world. It was simply showing up as someone who cares about their kitchen, their budget, and their peace of mind. In a market full of guilt, Cubby offered trust instead.
